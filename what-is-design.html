What is Design?

Essential principles and approaches to human experience.

Chance Castaneda

T 52 radio 1961

Our environment, due to a variety of factors, poses fundamental challenges to our human experience.

Design starts where these challenges begin.

The field is inevitably interdisciplinary, as there are an untold number useful things that could improve our lives. And as our innovations enable us to do new things, we encounter even more problems to address.

Design is both an art and science. It's an art simply because it comes from our imagination. It can be beautiful, functional, and delightful â€” and at times, deeply driven by an invisible intuition.

But it's also a science. There are methodologies and proven approaches.

Because it tackles problems, it can be assessed both quantitatively and qualitatively in terms of art, emotion, and vision, but also by fit, functionality, and usefulness.

But it's not perfect.

We're in the bussiness of human behavior, and our individual perceptions of a problem can lead to vastly different outcomes. This is why communication, often between designers, engineers, consumers, marketers, and management is so important.

Because design is a negotiation between our whims and the environment.

We're constantly experimenting, coming up with new ideas, prototypes, and approaches hoping that something will catch. That we'll discover something valuable to move us forward.

In this way, we're scientists. We're visionaries. We do the work that improves our lives in both bite-sized and enormous ways.

But not all tests provide us with good answers. And over time, pioneers in the field have developed general principles to keep our designs human, beautiful, and delightful.

The following principles and guidelines showcase some of my favorites, from low to high resolution.

There is no limit or end to design â€” because there is no limit or end to our imagination.

Braun Design 1972
Innovative

Act as if there is always a possibility of better.

Useful

Emphasize usefulness, and satisfy functional and aesthetic needs.

Aesthetic

Execute well to be beautiful; our well being depends on it.

Understandable

Clarify structure, and be easy to explain. Be obvious.

Unobstrusive

Stay neutral and restrained, and leave room for self-expression.

Honest

Do not manipulate with promises that cannot be kept.

Long-lasting

Prepare to live many years, so avoid being fashionable.

Detailed

Leave nothing to chance. Be accurate, never arbitrary.

Sustainable

Conserve resources, and reduce visual and physical pollution.

Less, but better

Concentrate on the essential; stay pure, stay simple.

Wayfinding

Be easy to navigate, especially in a hurry.

Feedback

Tell us when our actions had consequences.

Visibility

Make essential functions impossible to miss.

Consistency

Set expectations and stick to them everywhere.

Mental Model

Meet expectations or make new systems simple.

Proximity

Make controls next to what they change.

Grouping

Categorize by function or what makes sense.

Mapping

A slider should go up to make something raise.

Affordance

Indicate how something should be used.

Progressive Disclosure

Start simple; then iteratively go complex.

80/20 Rule

80% benifits from 20% of actions. Reduce clutter.

Symmetry

Optimize for visual harmony.
